Handmade Books by me

Sure, I think reading books is thrilling. Buying books, new or used, is it’s own thrill, too. But making books? That’s the ride I’ll stand in line for.
Here are a few lessons I've learned as I've undertaken to create my own books, zines and anthologies.


A Brief Example of Imposition

If you are creating a small book or zine of printed pages, it will be useful to understand a little about imposition. If you are printing pages, you have most likely created them with the aid of a computer, and to retrieve them from the computer, they must go through the printer. How can you ensure that the pages you want will make their journey in the right order? That’s imposition. It quickly gets confusing when your design includes folios, such that the printed paper must be folded, then the binding stitched or glued, to complete the book.

To take a closer look at imposition, I present a short book of haikus that I’ve made as an example. Everytime I begin a new print job for a book or zine, I return to this one. It’s just simple enough to understand what’s happening.

The book consists of eight pages, one folio. The printer prints four pages, and I fold them in half and lay them on top of one another.

Every even numbered page is set upside down. This is one trick I've learned when imposing my pages. The upside down text becomes the back of the previous page. It must be upside down, for this is the way the printer prints when it is told to print both sides of the page.
